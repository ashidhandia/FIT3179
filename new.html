Cost of AI Evolution: Progress or Pitfall?

With changing times, AI has swiftly integrated into our daily lives. Be it the virtual assistant on our phones or generative AI taking the whole world by storm, it is undeniable that artificial intelligence has come a long way. With evolutionary breakthroughs seemingly rolling out each month, artificial intelligence is growing stronger with each iteration. There are winners and losers for each achievement that comes our way. It's easy to get swept up in the waves of hype, but these shiny capabilities come at a real cost to society and our planet.

The 60s had the nuclear arms race where the major superpowers were competing in increasing their nuclear arsenals through rigorous research and high volumes of investment. The 21st century is now seeing its own version of the arms race with artificial intelligence. Countries have devoted trillions of dollars towards AI development and research to harness the true potential of artificial intelligence for boosting economic growth and technological advancement.

According to OurWorldInData, the United States alone invested around 150 billion dollars in 2021. For comparison, this is equivalent to the entire GDP of Morocco.

According to the research by Patterson et al. (2021), the average energy consumption can be calculated through a set formula. It depends heavily on the number of parameters, as the more parameters there are, the longer and more intensive the training process becomes:
Energy (kWh) = Hours to train × Number of processors × Average power per processor (kW) × 1000 ÷ PUE.
The formula highlights how various factors such as training duration, the number of processors, and the power consumption of each processor contribute to the overall energy demand of training AI models.

This overuse of electricity is certainly harmful, which brings us to the concept of Earth Overshoot Day. Earth Overshoot Day marks the point in the year when humanity has consumed more resources than the Earth can regenerate in that same year. Since the late 1970s, this day has crept earlier and earlier on the calendar, signaling an unsustainable strain on the planet's resources. In 2023, it fell on August 2nd, demonstrating that we are consuming 75% more than what Earth's ecosystems can replenish.
